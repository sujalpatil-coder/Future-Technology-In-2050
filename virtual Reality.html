About Virtual Reality

Virtual Reality (VR) is a computer-generated environment that simulates real-world or imaginary environments. It allows users to interact in 3D using headsets, sensors, and controllers. VR is widely used in gaming, education, healthcare, and training industries.


Virtual Reality (VR) is a computer-generated, three-dimensional simulated environment that allows users to interact with an artificial, immersive world using specialized hardware, such as head-mounted displays (HMDs) and sensory controllers. By replacing the user's physical surroundings with a digital, 360-degree experience, VR tricks the brain into feeling "present" in a non-physical space, enabling them to look around, move, and interact with objects in real-time. Unlike augmented reality (AR), which overlays digital content onto the real world, VR fully immerses the user in a totally constructed environment.

Components of VR

VR Headset

Displays immersive 3D visuals.


VR headsets are wearable devices that create immersive, 3D simulated environments for gaming, training, and entertainment by using dual displays and motion tracking. Modern headsets, such as standalone Meta Quest models or PC-based systems, use lenses and sensors to create a, 3D, stereoscopic effect providing a, 360-degree, interactive experience.
Key Types of VR Headsets : Standalone (All-in-One): These operate without a PC or console, using built-in processors and batteries for a portable experience (e.g., Meta Quest). Tethered (PC/Console VR): These connect to a high-performance computer or console for superior graphics and processing power (e.g., Bigscreen Beyond).

Sensors

Track motion and position.


Variable Reluctance (VR) sensors are passive, rugged magnetic devices used primarily to detect the speed and position of moving ferromagnetic metal components, such as gear teeth, shafts, or crankshafts. They generate their own AC voltage signal without external power, making them highly reliable in harsh, high-temperature automotive and industrial environments. Key Features and Principles :
Operating Principle: A permanent magnet creates a magnetic field. As a ferromagnetic target moves past the sensor's pole piece, it disrupts this field, inducing a voltage pulse in the coil. Passive Design: Being "self-generating," these sensors require no external voltage source, often featuring simple two-wire connections. Performance Limitations: They rely on speed; at very low rotational speeds, they may not generate a usable signal, and they cannot detect speed at zero velocity (stationary) unless a specific amplified type is used. Accuracy: Functionality depends on the "air gap" (distance between sensor and target), which is typically kept small (up to 3mm) to maintain signal strength. Common Applications Automotive: Crankshaft and camshaft position sensors, wheel speed sensors, and transmission speed sensing. Industrial: Measuring speed in motors, gears, turbines, and general position monitoring.

Controllers

Allow interaction with virtual world.


VR controllers are handheld input devices featuring motion tracking, buttons, and haptics, enabling interaction with virtual environments. They primarily use 6DoF (six-degrees-of-freedom) tracking to monitor both position and orientation. Standard controls include triggers for grabbing, joysticks for movement, and menus.
Key Aspects of VR Controllers:
Tracking Methods: Inside-Out: Cameras on the headset track the controllers (e.g., Meta Quest 3). Outside-In: External sensors or "lighthouses" track the controllers, offering high precision (e.g., Valve Index, Vive). Types of Movement: 6DoF (Six Degrees of Freedom): Tracks rotation (pitch, yaw, roll) and position (up/down, left/right, forward/backward). 3DoF (Three Degrees of Freedom): Tracks only rotation, commonly used in older or basic headsets.
