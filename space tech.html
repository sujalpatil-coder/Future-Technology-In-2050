Types of Space Technology

Satellites

satellite is an artificial object placed into orbit around a celestial body, primarily used for communication, weather forecasting, navigation, and Earth observation. They range from small, specialized instruments to large structures like the International Space Station, orbiting at various altitudes—typically Low Earth Orbit (LEO) or Geostationary Orbit (GEO)—to balance gravitational forces with speed.
Key Aspects of Satellite Information: Types of Orbits: Low Earth Orbit (LEO): Positioned 500–800 km high, used for imaging and Earth monitoring because they provide high-resolution data, though they move rapidly. Geostationary Orbit (GEO): Positioned at ~36,000 km, these orbit at the same rate Earth spins, allowing them to remain fixed over one location, ideal for television and weather monitoring. Medium Earth Orbit (MEO): Located between LEO and GEO (~5,000–20,000 km), commonly used for navigation systems like GPS. Main Components: Satellites usually have a bus (body), solar panels for power, antennae for transmitting signals, and onboard computers or sensors. Functions & Applications: Communication: Relaying television, phone, and internet signals around the globe. Observation: Tracking climate change, ocean temperatures, and mapping. Navigation: Providing GPS data. Military: Surveillance, reconnaissance, and early warning. Launch & Operation: Satellites are launched into space using rockets, which lift them vertically before placing them into specific orbits.

Rockets

A rocket is an elongated vehicle that uses a self-contained propulsion system to travel through the vacuum of space by ejecting high-speed exhaust gas. Operating on Newton’s third law of motion, rockets carry both fuel and oxidizer to create thrust, enabling them to launch payloads, satellites, and astronauts into orbit and beyond.
Key Aspects of Rockets: Propulsion System: Rockets carry their own oxygen (oxidizer) and fuel (solid or liquid), allowing them to operate outside the atmosphere, unlike jet engines. Four Main Systems: Rockets consist of a structural system (frame), propulsion system (engine/fuel), guidance system (computers/sensors), and payload system (cargo/crew). How They Work: Fuel burns in a chamber, creating hot gas that is pushed backward through a nozzle. The reaction produces a powerful, equal force that drives the rocket forward. Types & Uses: Rockets are used for space exploration, satellites, and military purposes (missiles). Famous examples include the Saturn V and SpaceX Falcon 9. History: The first rockets were used in China as early as the 1200s for fireworks and weapons, according to Wikipedia and NASA. Components & Structure Nozzle: Shapes and accelerates the exhaust gas. Payload: The cargo carried (e.g., satellite, space probe, humans). Guidance System: Uses computers, sensors, and cameras to steer the rocket. Propellant: Fuel and oxidizer combined. Britannica Britannica Common Propellant Types Liquid: Fuel (like hydrogen) and liquid oxygen are pumped into a combustion chamber. Solid: Fuel and oxidizer are mixed into a solid, rubbery substance. National Geographic Rockets are essential for space exploration because they are the only current technology capable of generating enough speed (escape velocity) to leave Earth's atmosphere

Space Stations

International Space Station - NASAThe International Space Station (ISS) is a modular, habitable artificial satellite in low Earth orbit, serving as a premier microgravity research laboratory since 2000. It is a joint project among five space agencies—NASA (USA), Roscosmos (Russia), JAXA (Japan), ESA (Europe), and CSA (Canada)—circling Earth every 90 minutes.
Key Facts About the ISS Location & Orbit: Orbits ~400 km (250 miles) above Earth. Size: Roughly the area of a football field (109 m long, 75 m wide), with a pressurized volume similar to a six-bedroom house. Speed: Travels at 28,000 km/h ( ), completing 16 orbits daily. Habitation: Continuously occupied since November 2000. It supports a crew of seven, offering sleeping quarters, gyms, and a 360-degree viewing bay window (Cupola). Purpose: Research in astrobiology, space medicine, and physical sciences to prepare for long-duration missions to the Moon and Mars. Operational Space Stations ISS: The primary international, multi-modular facility. Tiangong: China's space station, occupied since June 2022. Other Historical/Future Stations Salyut & Mir: Early Soviet/Russian stations, pioneered long-term habitation. Skylab: The first United States space station (1973–1979). Bharatiya Antariksha Station (BAS): India's planned space station, with initial modules scheduled for 2028 and human capability by 2035.Key Discoveries and Technology Human Health: Studies on bone/muscle atrophy in microgravity. Water Recycling: Advanced purification systems developed for space are used on Earth. Internet: Astronauts have full internet connectivity via onboard laptops.
